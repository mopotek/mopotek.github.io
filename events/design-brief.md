## 2. Hero Section

### Concept
The page opens with a scroll-triggered typographic animation built around the phrase:

> **"A PASSION FOR BRINGING PEOPLE TOGETHER"**

### Animation
- On load: the letters of **TOGETHER** are spread *wide apart* across the screen, each letter isolated. At the same time, **particle dots** are scattered across the screen — also spread far apart, like strangers in a room who don't know each other yet.
- As the user scrolls: the letters slide toward each other *and* the particles migrate closer simultaneously — two parallel convergences happening at once.
- On completion: the word **TOGETHER** is whole, the particles are now clustered densely around it — a crowd that found itself.

### Value 1 — *Clarity is hospitality*

**Illustration — directly under the value name:**
A small face illustration: anxious/worried → relaxed/happy. This sits right beneath the title as an immediate visual anchor for the transformation this value creates.

**Principle:**
> In my experience, planning for the most anxious person in the room is the single most effective thing you can do as an event organiser. When the person with the most questions feels completely taken care of, everyone else does too — and people arrive on time, confident, and ready to connect.

This isn't about catering to anxiety — it's about the smartest efficiency move in events. Nail the details for the hardest attendee to reassure, and you've nailed it for everyone.

**Animation — "The Worry Cloud":**
- The value name sits at the centre of the section.
- Floating *around* it, in varying sizes and slight rotational wobble (shaking anxiously), are the real attendee worries:
  - *"Will I be too early?"*
  - *"Where do I park?"*
  - *"What time does it start?"*
  - *"How many people will be there?"*
  - *"What's the dress code?"*
  - *"Will I know anyone?"*
  - *"What if I'm hungry?"*
- The value statement itself starts muted — low opacity, small.
- As the user scrolls:
  - The anxiety questions **shrink** in font size
  - Their colour **fades** to low-contrast and barely legible
  - They drift outward
  - The **value statement grows** — bolder, higher contrast, fully visible — clarity cutting through noise
- End state: strong, confident statement. The worries are still technically present but almost invisible — *dealt with*, not erased.

### Value 2 — *Make it electric*

> Fun, immersive, meaningful — three things every event I organise has to be.

Copy beneath the title clarifies each dimension:
- **Fun** — the room should feel alive. People shouldn't have to try to enjoy themselves; the event should do it for them.
- **Immersive** — design an environment people can fall into. Give them something to do, not just something to watch.
- **Meaningful** — help people take something away: a connection, a feeling, a memory. Entertainment that leaves no trace isn't enough.

#### Fun — The FUN Platform Game Animation
- Text reads: **"Make it"** then the word **FUN** appears as large, bold capital letters — but they're *platforms*.
- A small animated character runs across the screen:
  - Jumps up onto the **F** — lands on the flat top
  - Runs across and leaps to the **U** — slides down inside the curve of the U like a halfpipe or skateboard bowl, picking up speed
  - Gets launched out of the U toward the **N** — hits the N running, then launches *off the screen* (jumps away from the viewer, into the distance)
- The whole sequence is energetic, fast, joyful — it *is* fun, not just a description of it.

#### Immersive — Anchoring Activities
Concept: a well-designed anchoring activity gives people something to *do* the moment they arrive — it pulls them in and, once engaged, propels them into energy and conversation. Like a ship's anchor in water: it holds people in place long enough to find their footing, then the momentum builds.

**Animation:**
- An anchor image sways gently from side to side on screen.
- As the user scrolls, the swaying gains momentum — the arc grows wider and faster, like a pendulum building energy.
- The motion conveys the idea without being too literal: the anchor isn't about being stuck — it's about the *momentum* that a well-placed focal point creates.

#### Meaningful — The Character Arc
- A small round character stands with arms at sides, neutral face.
- As the user scrolls:
  - Character begins to smile
  - Then blushes
  - Then transitions into the 🤗 hug emoji — arms open, heart visible
- Simultaneously: the word **"meaningful"** in the copy highlights — turns red (or a warm accent colour) and becomes bold
- The message: help people take something away — a feeling, a connection, a memory.

### Value 3 — *Capture the feeling*

> Capture the good moments — especially early, before people realise they're being documented.

**Copy direction:**
There's something deeply gratifying about seeing a great photo of yourself at an event. It makes you remember how good that moment felt — and you want to share it. That instinct is powerful: for attendees, it's a way to hold onto the experience; for companies and organisers, it's organic visibility. A well-captured event communicates itself. It helps brands and teams show the world what their culture looks and feels like — without a marketing brief.

**Interactive element — The Selfie Capture Tool:**
This value section includes an embedded webcam selfie tool directly in the page — a mini version of exactly what Alexandra facilitates at real events.

Flow:
1. A prompt invites the visitor: *"Take one right now."*
2. User clicks → browser requests webcam permission
3. Countdown or capture button
4. On click: **camera shutter sound effect** plays
5. The photo appears in a **Polaroid frame** — white border, slight tilt, drop shadow — physical and warm
6. A short line appears beneath it: *"This is what I create space for. A captured moment becomes a shared one — and a shared one travels."*

**Privacy hint copy** (displayed before or beneath the camera prompt):
> *Your photo isn't sent anywhere. It's stored locally in your browser and disappears when you close this page.*

The interaction turns a passive value statement into something the visitor *lives through* — they become a participant in the very thing being described.
