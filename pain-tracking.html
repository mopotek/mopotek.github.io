A pain-tracking companion that empowers its users

2017

Product definition • UX Writing • UX design

The problem

Millions of people suffer from physical pain that handicaps their personal and professional life, causing a great deal of mental suffering as well dissociation of the person from their body.

The companion was designed to allow individuals suffering from chronic pain to track their pain’s evolution everyday in a way that empowers them to become experts of their bodies and their pain.

This was a particularly relevant project because previous interviews with chronic pain afflicted people showed me that when one gained a sense of ownership over their pain, it made it way more tolerable mentally and gave the individual a stronger sense of agency.

The objectives

  • Make the users feel heard and understood: they are the experts of their own pain
  • Incorporate several pain-scaling questionnaires into an app
  • Allow the users to get feedback in a Quantified Self Approach
  • Make the terms and conditions as transparent and GDPR friendly as possible

Which methods did I use?

This project was under an NDA contract so I cannot disclose precise elements of the project.

1. Product definition with card-sorting exercise and userflows

The research team and the product owner gave me a list of functionalities and user stories that they deduced from interviews with people that have chronic pain.

From there, I created an app architecture that I deduced from card-sorting exercises I carried out with several users where each card was a functionality.

That way, the architecture that I defined was consistent with the user's mental models. I concretized this step with userflows that made this architecture easy to understand for all stakeholders of the project.

2. Gamification design

The app was gamified in order to make it more pleasant and encourage the user to use it daily for more accurate tracking.

I created a multi-level gamification system that rewarded the user for performing crucial actions such as tracking their pain or filling out their medical profile for a more accurate analysis.

3. Lo / hi-res prototyping and user testing

First, I created paper prototypes that I tested with internal staff which revealed me some usability in the interface, and GDPR comprehension issues in the wording that led me to create an A and B versions of the consenting process. This process was paper prototyped and tested again, which saved a lot of resources that we would have used if we had realised these issues later.

I then created hi-fidelity dynamic prototypes with Axure (after one more round of testing and redesigning) that allowed to concretize the app definition to deliver to the stakeholders and developers.

Particular attention was put on the user's consent by being transparent on which data we collected and why by using exact understandable wording.
