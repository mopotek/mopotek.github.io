My AI OS

I don't just use AI. I built a system that knows me.

Most people use AI like they Google things - ask a question, get an answer, move on. I built a personal assistant with full context on everything I'm working on, so every output is actually relevant to my situation - not generic filler.

🧠 Personal OS Architecture Diagram Central AI hub with spokes to: Projects, Stakeholders, Slack/Teams, Writing Style, Repeatable Skills, External Sources (Drive, Confluence)

What lives in my OS

  • All active projects and their context
  • Stakeholders - who they are, roles, communication style
  • My writing style and preferred tone
  • Repeatable skills - e.g. a workflow that analyzes user interviews the same way every time
  • Links to sources of truth - it knows to pull a specific Drive folder before drafting a strategy, so the output is grounded in real context

Voice-first with Wispr Flow

I don't type anymore - I use Wispr Flow, a voice transcription app. One keypress and everything I say is instantly transcribed, anywhere I'm typing.

Counterintuitive insight

Typing actually restricts your thinking. When you type, you shortcut the cognitive process. When you speak, you're forced to reason out loud first. AI's job is just to capture and format those thoughts - not to create them for you.

Skills of the Future

You don't need to be a tech expert. But some things are becoming non-negotiable.

Personal observations from my experience - not a universal prescription. Click any card to read the full thought.

Before - The process-first world

Following the right steps was enough. User research → brainstorm → ideation → wireframes → implementation. Each phase: weeks. Completing the steps correctly = doing your job well.

Now - The outcome-first world

All those steps now take 20 minutes. What companies care about: Did you have the right strategy? Did it deliver results? The process is table stakes. Outcomes are everything.

🎯 Outcomes over process ▾
The quiet disappearance of roles that follow process without creating meaningful impact.

AI has made process execution fast and cheap. If your work is mostly about completing steps correctly rather than producing real impact, that work is increasingly automatable. The employees who thrive will be the ones who can identify what outcome is actually needed and use AI to get there. Not those who just execute tasks well.

🔌 Getting (gently) technical ▾
Building your own productivity system requires getting comfortable with configuration - not as scary as it sounds.

Non-technical employees will increasingly need to: set up their own AI OS, create automations between tools, connect third-party services. What is an MCP? Think of it as a "plug" that lets your AI tool read from and write to other systems - your HR software, your calendar - without copy-pasting. The goal isn't to become a developer. It's to be less afraid of configuration.

⚙️ Automation literacy ▾
Spotting where technology can close the gap - less about coding, more about systems thinking.

A lot of jobs today are largely: inputting data, connecting data from different places, reporting on data. These are exactly what AI automates best. The skill that will matter: being able to look at a workflow and ask "which of these steps should a human actually be doing?" This is process analysis, not programming.

🧠 Critical thinking & cognitive resilience ▾
The effects of AI on cognitive decline are starting to be documented. Heavy chatbot use correlates with reduced strategic thinking.

If you outsource your thinking to AI, you risk atrophying the judgment muscles that make you good at your job. The key skill: knowing the difference between using AI to support non-strategic work vs. letting it replace your thinking. My practice: voice dictation forces me to form a thought before asking AI. It captures and formats my thinking - it doesn't create it.

💬 Prompt craft ▾
How you communicate with AI determines the quality of what you get back. This is a learnable, high-ROI skill.

When asking AI to review a contract, tell it not just what to change - but also what should stay exactly as it is, to avoid hallucinations. Always include examples of good and bad outputs. Give it context about who you are and what "good" looks like. The better your prompt, the less time you spend editing the output.

New roles companies will need

🏆 AI Champions

People who have done the work themselves - not just read about it. Good teachers and advocates who create genuine energy around AI adoption. They're not "the AI person" - they're a colleague who can show you how they actually do it. They lead by example, not by title.

🔄 Change Managers for AI

Rolling out a fundamental change to how people work, at speed, across a whole organization - this is an industrial revolution. You cannot expect people to figure it out on their own. Dedicated change management support is essential to bring people along and make the transition stick.

AI in Your HR World

From a prompt library to a system that runs itself.

A note before we start

I don't have deep HR expertise - what follows is one way to think about this based on what's technically possible. Think of it as a starting point, not a blueprint. I'd love to hear from you whether it resonates with your real challenges.

A well-crafted list of AI prompts is a great first step. But it's still manual - you have to remember to use it, find the right prompt, paste in the context, and act on the output. Every single time.

The next level is designing a system where most of this just happens - triggered automatically, context already loaded, output delivered where you need it.

Important caveat

AI in HR is powerful for: drafting, structuring, analyzing, synthesizing, prioritizing, communicating, standardizing. It should not replace: legal validation, termination decisions, collective labor matters, country-specific compliance interpretation. Use AI to free up your time for exactly those higher-judgment calls - and always validate locally when required.

Scaling AI

Individual productivity isn't enough. You need a system.

Based on what I've seen across multiple tech companies - including Atlassian directly. Here's what actually works when you're trying to move an entire organization.

Training principles that work

01
Build it internally

Examples in your training must be relevant to people's actual work. Internal creators also become points of contact - employees can follow up directly. External vendors can't do this.

02
Bite-sized & immediate

1–2 minute videos, one tool or workflow each. Low barrier to watch. Always followed by immediate hands-on practice. People learn by doing, not watching.

03
Psychological safety

People need to feel competent, not just informed. Design for confidence through repetition and community - not just tool exposure. Fear and uncertainty are the real blockers.

The AI Builder Week model - click between days

Every 3–6 months, a 4-day intensive with 4-hour workshops each day.

🌟 Inspire

Examples from the real world

Show the best AI use cases from across the company - not vendor demos, but real people in your organization doing real things. Add examples from outside the company too.

Introduce new tools and workflows you want people to try. Energy-building mode - not tutorial mode. No slides full of features. Stories of impact.

📚 Learn

Hands-on tutorials

Step-by-step tutorials on the specific tools introduced on Day 1 - led by someone who actually uses them daily, not an external trainer.

For global teams: run the same session across multiple time zones. Require prep work beforehand - e.g. a vibe-coded setup guide so people configure their machines in advance. No wasted session time on setup.

🔨 Build

Work on something real

The most important day. Small groups take a project they're currently working on and optimize it with AI. The output is directly relevant to their actual work.

Key principle: it's not extra time away from work - it is their work, in an experimental setting. They leave with something they can use on Monday. This matters for buy-in at every level of the organization.

🏆 Showcase

Share, celebrate, sustain

Groups present what they built. Recognition and awards. Momentum to carry forward. This creates the internal story your organization tells itself about AI - built from real people doing real work.

These showcases also surface your next wave of champions. The people who built something exciting on Day 3 are your best recruiters for the next cohort.

Champions & communities of practice

After big training events, communities of practice keep the momentum alive - regular workshops focused on what people struggled with most, so they can get repetitions in and build real confidence.

Champions Network: Identify the highest AI tool users. Invite them to run weekly office hours. Make them go-to facilitators for future training. They lead by example - as colleagues, not "the AI person."

Async sharing channels: A dedicated Teams channel where people post short screen recordings of experiments. Reactions and comments create organic momentum. Normalise experimentation and make it visible across the org.

The insight

Making one person dramatically more productive with AI is easy. But if the next step in the process hasn't been sped up too, you've just moved the bottleneck. AI adoption at scale requires thinking about the whole workflow - not just individual roles. This requires cross-functional coordination, which is exactly why HR and ops leaders are so central to getting this right.

Closing thought

This is a change management problem as much as a technology problem. You're not rolling out a new tool - you're fundamentally changing how people work. Treat it accordingly and you'll get results. Treat it like a software deployment and you'll have a very expensive shelf product.
